Original

SAFA Prompt

A general-purpose rubric grading prompt. Tells the AI to be an "expert teacher" and follow a rubric with grading bands.

✓Rubric-based marking — grades across multiple dimensions using defined bands
✓Formative feedback — encourages affirmative, encouraging tone
✗No evidence locking — doesn't require quoting the student's words before judging
✗No syllabus anchoring — doesn't restrict AI to concepts the student actually wrote about
✗No scope boundaries — feedback can drift to unrelated topics
✗No self-check step — AI returns output without verifying its own accuracy

How the Markly AI Prompt Works

Step 1

🎓 AI Takes On the Role of Examiner

The AI is told to act as a Singapore Geography teacher grading JC-level essays.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI is given a specific persona: a Singaporean Geography examiner from the Ministry of Education. This means it uses British English, understands the local syllabus, and knows the standard expected of 17–18 year-old students.
Step 2

📥 Receive the Inputs

The AI receives three things: the student's essay, the essay question, and the grading criteria.

Click to learn more ↓
Before doing any grading, the AI is given:
  • The student's essay — the actual text the student wrote
  • Assessment Objectives (AOs) — the learning goals the essay is judged against
  • Syllabus pointers — a list of specific Geography topics the essay could cover
Rule 1

🔒 Evidence Locking — Only Grade What's Written

The AI is strictly forbidden from guessing what the student "meant to say." It can only judge actual written words.

Click to learn more ↓
Safety rules for the AI:
  • Must quote the student's exact words before making any judgement
  • Cannot assume the student knows something they didn't write down
  • Cannot say something is "missing" unless it has scanned the entire essay and confirmed it's truly absent
  • Cannot introduce new Geography concepts the student didn't mention
Example: If a student doesn't mention "carbon release," the AI cannot say "You forgot about carbon release." It can only comment on what the student did write.
Step 3

🔍 Select Up to 5 Quotes from the Essay

The AI picks up to five specific sentences from the essay to give feedback on.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI reads the whole essay and selects the most important passages — up to five. Each selected quote must be an exact copy of what the student wrote.
The AI focuses on the 5 highest-impact feedback only.
Step 4

🏷️ Classify Each Quote

For each quote, the AI decides what type of writing it is — description, explanation, evidence, or evaluation.

Click to learn more ↓
This matters because different types of writing need different feedback:
  • Descriptive statement — States a fact without explaining why
  • Causal explanation — Explains why or how something happens
  • Example / Evidence — A specific case or data point
  • Evaluation — A judgement weighing significance
Key rule: The AI must not treat a description as if it were an explanation. A student who writes "Deforestation causes flooding" is describing, not explaining how.
Step 5

🎯 Match to Assessment Objective

Each quote is linked to a specific learning goal (AO) to ensure the feedback is curriculum-aligned.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI checks: "Which learning goal does this quote relate to?"
  • The AI must justify why the quote is relevant to that AO
  • If it can't justify the link, it must not use that quote
Rule 2

📎 Syllabus Pointer Must Match the Quote

The AI can only tag a syllabus topic if the student's own words support it.

Click to learn more ↓
This prevents the AI from "inventing" connections to the syllabus:
  • The syllabus topic must be directly reflected in the student's actual words
  • The AI must quote the exact phrase that supports the link
  • If no phrase supports the link, the AI must not use that syllabus pointer
Example: If the student writes about "trees being removed causing rain to wash away topsoil," the AI can tag "soil erosion." But it cannot tag "loss of biodiversity" from that same sentence.
Rule 3

🧱 Stay Within the Quote's Scope

Feedback on a quote must only address ideas in that quote — not ideas from other paragraphs.

Click to learn more ↓
  • The AI must not pull in ideas from other paragraphs unless it explicitly labels them as a "cross-reference" with a direct quote
  • Each feedback comment stays focused on that one highlighted passage
For students to know exactly which sentence the feedback applies to and can revise precisely.
Step 6

✍️ Write Balanced Feedback

For each quote, the AI writes feedback that acknowledges strengths and gives concrete next steps.

Click to learn more ↓
The feedback follows a structured teaching model:
  • Feed Up — Remind the student of the goal and what "good" looks like
  • Feed Back — Say what the student did well and identify the most important gap
  • Feed Forward — Give a specific, actionable next step to improve
Good feedback sounds like: "You have explained that deforestation leads to soil erosion — this could be further strengthened by explaining how the removal of root systems specifically reduces soil stability."
Rule 4

❓ Stay Aligned to the Essay Question

All suggestions must help the student answer the specific essay question better — not drift to unrelated topics.

Click to learn more ↓
If the essay question asks "How does deforestation affect LDCs?", then every improvement suggestion must help the student explain impacts on Less Developed Countries — not drift into general ecosystem science.
This prevents the AI from suggesting the student write about something impressive-sounding but irrelevant to the question.
Quality Check

✅ Self-Check Before Submitting

Before returning any results, the AI runs a mandatory 5-point quality checklist on its own work.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI asks itself:
  • Did I only use approved Assessment Objectives? (If not → abort entirely)
  • Does every syllabus tag match the student's actual words? (If not → remove it)
  • Did I introduce any new concepts the student didn't mention? (If so → remove them)
  • Does the student already address this point elsewhere? (If so → acknowledge it)
  • Is every claim I make backed by a direct quote?
This is like a teacher double-checking their red-pen comments before handing back the paper.

What it checks

Feedback Mechanics

Does feedback follow the Feed Up / Feed Back / Feed Forward structure?

What it checks
