System Prompts

The two AI instructions being compared. Both tell the AI how to grade student essays — but with very different levels of control.

Original

SAFA Prompt

A general-purpose rubric grading prompt. Tells the AI to be an "expert teacher" and follow a rubric with grading bands.

✓Rubric-based marking — grades across multiple dimensions using defined bands
✓Formative feedback — encourages affirmative, encouraging tone
✗No evidence locking — doesn't require quoting the student's words before judging
✗No syllabus anchoring — doesn't restrict AI to concepts the student actually wrote about
✗No scope boundaries — feedback can drift to unrelated topics
✗No self-check step — AI returns output without verifying its own accuracy
New

Markly AI Prompt

A highly controlled, evidence-locked grading prompt built for Singapore Geography. Multiple safety rules prevent common AI feedback errors.

✓Evidence locking — AI must quote the student's exact words before making any judgement
✓Syllabus anchoring — syllabus tags must be supported by phrases in the student's essay
✓Scope boundaries — feedback stays within the quoted text; cross-references must be labelled
✓Question focus alignment — all suggestions must strengthen the student's answer to the specific question
✓Feed Up / Back / Forward style — structured feedback modelled on MOE guidelines
✓Mandatory self-check — AI verifies its own output against 5 criteria before returning

How the Markly AI Prompt Works

Step 1

🎓 AI Takes On the Role of Examiner

The AI is told to act as a Singapore Geography teacher grading JC-level essays.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI is given a specific persona: a Singaporean Geography examiner from the Ministry of Education. This means it uses British English, understands the local syllabus, and knows the standard expected of 17–18 year-old students.
Step 2

📥 Receive the Inputs

The AI receives three things: the student's essay, the essay question, and the grading criteria.

Click to learn more ↓
Before doing any grading, the AI is given:
  • The student's essay — the actual text the student wrote
  • Assessment Objectives (AOs) — the learning goals the essay is judged against
  • Syllabus pointers — a list of specific Geography topics the essay could cover
Rule 1

🔒 Evidence Locking — Only Grade What's Written

The AI is strictly forbidden from guessing what the student "meant to say." It can only judge actual written words.

Click to learn more ↓
Safety rules for the AI:
  • Must quote the student's exact words before making any judgement
  • Cannot assume the student knows something they didn't write down
  • Cannot say something is "missing" unless it has scanned the entire essay and confirmed it's truly absent
  • Cannot introduce new Geography concepts the student didn't mention
Example: If a student doesn't mention "carbon release," the AI cannot say "You forgot about carbon release." It can only comment on what the student did write.
Step 3

🔍 Select Up to 5 Quotes from the Essay

The AI picks up to five specific sentences from the essay to give feedback on.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI reads the whole essay and selects the most important passages — up to five. Each selected quote must be an exact copy of what the student wrote.
The AI focuses on the 5 highest-impact feedback only.
Step 4

🏷️ Classify Each Quote

For each quote, the AI decides what type of writing it is — description, explanation, evidence, or evaluation.

Click to learn more ↓
This matters because different types of writing need different feedback:
  • Descriptive statement — States a fact without explaining why
  • Causal explanation — Explains why or how something happens
  • Example / Evidence — A specific case or data point
  • Evaluation — A judgement weighing significance
Key rule: The AI must not treat a description as if it were an explanation. A student who writes "Deforestation causes flooding" is describing, not explaining how.
Step 5

🎯 Match to Assessment Objective

Each quote is linked to a specific learning goal (AO) to ensure the feedback is curriculum-aligned.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI checks: "Which learning goal does this quote relate to?"
  • The AI must justify why the quote is relevant to that AO
  • If it can't justify the link, it must not use that quote
Rule 2

📎 Syllabus Pointer Must Match the Quote

The AI can only tag a syllabus topic if the student's own words support it.

Click to learn more ↓
This prevents the AI from "inventing" connections to the syllabus:
  • The syllabus topic must be directly reflected in the student's actual words
  • The AI must quote the exact phrase that supports the link
  • If no phrase supports the link, the AI must not use that syllabus pointer
Example: If the student writes about "trees being removed causing rain to wash away topsoil," the AI can tag "soil erosion." But it cannot tag "loss of biodiversity" from that same sentence.
Rule 3

🧱 Stay Within the Quote's Scope

Feedback on a quote must only address ideas in that quote — not ideas from other paragraphs.

Click to learn more ↓
  • The AI must not pull in ideas from other paragraphs unless it explicitly labels them as a "cross-reference" with a direct quote
  • Each feedback comment stays focused on that one highlighted passage
For students to know exactly which sentence the feedback applies to and can revise precisely.
Step 6

✍️ Write Balanced Feedback

For each quote, the AI writes feedback that acknowledges strengths and gives concrete next steps.

Click to learn more ↓
The feedback follows a structured teaching model:
  • Feed Up — Remind the student of the goal and what "good" looks like
  • Feed Back — Say what the student did well and identify the most important gap
  • Feed Forward — Give a specific, actionable next step to improve
Good feedback sounds like: "You have explained that deforestation leads to soil erosion — this could be further strengthened by explaining how the removal of root systems specifically reduces soil stability."
Rule 4

❓ Stay Aligned to the Essay Question

All suggestions must help the student answer the specific essay question better — not drift to unrelated topics.

Click to learn more ↓
If the essay question asks "How does deforestation affect LDCs?", then every improvement suggestion must help the student explain impacts on Less Developed Countries — not drift into general ecosystem science.
This prevents the AI from suggesting the student write about something impressive-sounding but irrelevant to the question.
Quality Check

✅ Self-Check Before Submitting

Before returning any results, the AI runs a mandatory 5-point quality checklist on its own work.

Click to learn more ↓
The AI asks itself:
  • Did I only use approved Assessment Objectives? (If not → abort entirely)
  • Does every syllabus tag match the student's actual words? (If not → remove it)
  • Did I introduce any new concepts the student didn't mention? (If so → remove them)
  • Does the student already address this point elsewhere? (If so → acknowledge it)
  • Is every claim I make backed by a direct quote?
This is like a teacher double-checking their red-pen comments before handing back the paper.

✓ Yes → Output results
✗ No → Fix or abort

Evaluation Prompts

Five LLM-as-Judge evaluators that automatically score each AI output. Each checks a different quality dimension. Click any card to expand.

🔍

Evidence Grounding

Are all claims traceable to specific quotes in the student's essay?

Click for details ↓

What it checks

  • Direct quote verification — any claim about what the student wrote or didn't write must be verifiable through exact quotes or paraphrases from the essay
  • No unsubstantiated interpretations — feedback must not infer the student's understanding, intent, or comprehension beyond what is explicitly written
  • Contextual completeness — the entire essay must be evaluated before claiming something is absent; it may appear elsewhere or be implicitly addressed
  • Accurate attribution — praise or critique must accurately reflect what the student actually wrote; don't credit analysis they didn't provide

Violation examples

  • False positive attribution: "Your mention of X shows a clear understanding of Y" — when the student never mentioned X
  • False negative omission: "Consider elaborating on how X occurs" — when the student already explained it elsewhere in the essay
  • Unverifiable interpretation: "This shows you understand the concept of..." — inferring comprehension not evidenced in the text
  • Selective context ignoring: "The connection between X and Y needs clarification" — when the student addressed it in a different paragraph
  • Quote misrepresentation: "Your discussion of X..." — when the characterisation doesn't accurately reflect what was written
📎

Dataset Scope Alignment

Is the syllabus pointer supported by text in the student's essay?

Click for details ↓

What it checks

  • Syllabus grounding — feedback must only reference content explicitly present in the syllabus provided in the input prompt; no new concepts or frameworks should be introduced
  • Content alignment — feedback must directly address the specific knowledge reference in the syllabus, not suggest alternative or tangential topics
  • Targeted improvement guidance — suggestions should enhance the student's response within the existing scope (e.g., quality of causal explanation), not redirect toward unrelated syllabus content
  • Scope boundaries — when a student's response doesn't address part of the syllabus, feedback should clarify the gap rather than treat it as a required deficiency

Violation examples

  • Out-of-scope topic introduction: Suggesting discussion of "biogeochemical cycles" when the syllabus and student response focus on economic causation
  • Scope creep in requirements: "Minimum requirement: Address how disruption of biogeochemical cycles impacts sustainability" — when this topic isn't in the syllabus
  • Indirect suggestion masquerading as gap-filling: "To improve, discuss potential environmental trade-offs" — when environmental impacts aren't part of the syllabus
  • Conflation of partial relevance: Acknowledging a topic wasn't in the student's response, then still framing its inclusion as necessary for improvement despite being out of scope
🎯

Construct Alignment

Does the feedback correctly match the assessment objective it references?

Click for details ↓

What it checks

  • Construct-content alignment — the AO referenced in feedback must directly correspond to the actual quality issue in the student's response, grounded in the provided assessment objectives (not invented)
  • Holistic response evaluation — the AI must evaluate the full response relative to rubrics, not isolate individual elements or demand dimensions outside the AO's scope
  • Precise problem identification — the root cause of any deficiency must be accurately diagnosed before improvement suggestions are offered
  • Proportionate feedback scope — suggestions should prioritise the most significant rubric-based weaknesses, not peripheral enhancements

Violation examples

  • Construct misidentification: Referencing "Quality of Evaluation" when the response demonstrates analytical weakness, not evaluation weakness
  • Partial response analysis: Criticising lack of specificity while ignoring that the response already meets core rubric criteria across other dimensions
  • Root cause misdiagnosis: Suggesting more detailed support when the real issue is incomplete reasoning, not lack of evidence
  • Misaligned improvement recommendations: Recommending deeper analysis of carbon cycles when the student already addresses the core construct well
  • Construct conflation: Using "Evaluation" and "Analysis" interchangeably when they represent distinct rubric dimensions
✍️

Feedback Style

Does feedback follow the Feed Up / Feed Back / Feed Forward structure?

Click for details ↓

What it checks

  • Evidence-based specificity — all claims must be grounded in concrete evidence from the student's work, with direct references to specific passages or sentences
  • Actionable improvement guidance — feedback must include clear, executable next steps specific enough to guide revision while preserving student agency
  • Pedagogical independence — feedback should build metacognitive skills through checkpoint questions and self-reflection prompts, not just provide answers
  • Balanced strength-gap analysis — both strengths and gaps must be identified; exclusively deficit-focused or exclusively praise-focused feedback is a violation
  • Measurable clarity — guidance must use clear criteria so students can objectively verify whether they have met the suggested improvements

Violation examples

  • Unsupported claims: "Your writing is unclear" — no specific passage or sentence cited
  • Vague improvement suggestions: "Try to improve your writing style" — not specific or actionable enough to guide revision
  • Prescriptive over generative: "You should write: 'The protagonist's motivation stems from...'" — provides the answer instead of guiding the student to find it
  • Imbalanced evaluation: Multiple paragraphs of criticism with no acknowledgment of what was done well
  • Non-measurable guidance: "Add more depth to your analysis" — no criteria for what depth looks like or how to verify it
❓

Question Focus Alignment

Do all improvements directly strengthen the student's answer to the essay question?

Click for details ↓

What it checks

  • Direct question alignment — feedback must address the specific focus and requirements of the essay question without introducing tangential concepts
  • Conceptual boundary maintenance — when a question asks about specific impacts (e.g., effects on LDCs), feedback must not shift toward underlying mechanisms unless the question explicitly requires it
  • Improvement relevance — suggested improvements must enhance the student's response within the question's scope; adding unrelated detail constitutes scope creep
  • Contextual appropriateness — feedback should recognise what the question is testing; background knowledge should only be invoked when directly relevant

Violation examples

  • Conceptual redirection: "You could discuss how ecosystem disruption leads to haze" — when the question asks about effects on LDCs, not ecosystem mechanics
  • Scope creep: "A stronger version would explore how biodiversity loss worsens these conditions" — expanding beyond what the question requires
  • Mechanistic distraction: "Emphasise the causal relationship between deforestation and ecosystem disruption" — when the question asks about effects, not mechanisms
  • Knowledge reference mismatch: Tagging "disruption of ecosystems and loss of biodiversity" as the knowledge reference when the question asks about effects on LDCs
  • Prerequisite confusion: "First clarify how ecosystem disruption directly connects to health issues" — treating background context as the main focus

Questions & Scoring

Both prompts were tested on the same two essay questions using 25 student essays each.

Question 2A

"Explain how tropical deforestation may affect countries at a low level of development."

Question 2B

"It is not possible to stop tropical deforestation. How far do you agree?"

How were scores calculated?

Each of the 5 evaluators produces a binary score for every AI output: 1.000 = Pass 0.000 = Fail

The final score per evaluator is the average across all 25 student essays for each question. A score of 0.6480 means the AI passed on roughly 16 out of 25 essays for that evaluator.
